Freedom Corner

Freedom Corner stands today as a poignant testament to the resilience and collective spirit of the Hill District community. Located at the intersection of Center and Crawford streets, this monument holds profound significance as a symbol of resistance against urban renewal and a beacon of hope for social justice. Designed by architect Howard Graves, a native of the Hill District, Freedom Corner overlooks the once-vibrant Lower Hill, now a somber reminder of broken promises and displaced communities. With its floor crafted from opaque black stone from Zimbabwe, symbolizing the African heritage of many residents, and adorned with concentric rings bearing the names of fallen heroes and living advocates of civil rights, the monument serves as a perpetual tribute to those who dared to challenge the status quo. Dedicated on April 23rd, 2001, after two years of fundraising efforts led by Councilman Sala Udin, Freedom Corner embodies the enduring legacy of struggle and solidarity in the pursuit of justice.

The genesis of Freedom Corner traces back to the early 1960s, when James McCoy, former chair of the NAACP labor and industry committee, christened the site to galvanize the community's resistance against encroaching urban development. Named strategically, the designation of 'Freedom Corner' served not only as a physical marker but also as a rallying cry for the preservation of the Hill District's identity amidst the expansion of the Civic Arena. McCoy, alongside Frankie Pace, orchestrated protests in 1968 to oppose the further advancement of the arena into the Hill, anchoring their struggle in the broader context of the national Civil Rights movement. Backed by St. Benedict the Moor church, a steadfast supporter providing hospitality and sustenance to protestors, Freedom Corner became a focal point for dissent, symbolizing the collective determination to safeguard the community's rights and heritage. Thus, the naming of Freedom Corner not only imbued the site with symbolic significance but also strengthened the resolve of the Hill District residents to assert their voice and preserve their neighborhood's integrity.
